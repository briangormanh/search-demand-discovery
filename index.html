The premise

Keyword tools give you volumes. Search Console gives you voices.

Google Search Console is the one place you can read the actual words real customers use — including the thousands of long, spoken questions no keyword tool will ever show you.

Voice search and AI assistants trained people to ask full questions instead of typing two words. Those questions land in Search Console as raw, unrounded, deeply human language.

Read one at a time, they look like noise. Aggregated, they stop being keywords and start being jobs people are trying to get done — some of which a business has never thought to say out loud.

Our discovery process is simply learning to listen to them at scale.

The method

Five moves, from a raw query log to a page you can change on Monday.

This is a real sequence — each step depends on the one before it.

01

Read the whole landscape first

Pull the top queries by impression to see the shape of demand: what's branded, what's not, and where the volume actually sits versus where the business ranks.

02

Isolate the conversational queries

Spoken-style and AI-assistant queries are longer and carry natural-language markers. We pull them at scale, one marker at a time, keeping the page each question already lands on.

03

Cluster into jobs, not keywords

Group the questions by the human need underneath them — why someone is really asking — rather than by matching words. Seven distinct jobs surfaced.

04

Map each job to the page catching it

Every cluster already lands somewhere. We check whether that page actually answers the question — or whether the demand is leaking onto a blog post that can't convert.

05

Pick the lightest action that wins

For each job: a callout in an existing hero, a new section, or a brand-new page. Start with the smallest change that captures the demand.

What we heard

Seven things customers kept asking — in their own words.

Every query below is real and unedited. Only the brand name has been removed.

Theme 01 · the headline insight

Boarding is a travel purchase — so people search "near the airport."

What they're really asking: "I'm flying out. Where do I drop the dog on the way?" Proximity to the airport is a booking driver, not a location footnote.

dog boarding near airport
dog boarding by airport
pet boarding near airport
airport pet hotel
can i bring my dog to the airport to pick someone up
pet relief area atlanta airport
What we did

Branded "near the airport" searches alone drew 9,000+ impressions a year. We turned it into a hero callout on the airport-adjacent location, a travel FAQ, and a dedicated "board near the airport" page — then flagged the same play for every other location near a major airport.

Theme 02 · reassurance

"Is it a kennel — or something better?"

The job: confirm this is humane and safe before trusting it. People carry a negative "kennel" picture in their heads.

what is a kennel
what is dog boarding
kennel vs boarding
what is a boarding kennel
What we did

"what is a kennel" alone pulls ~17k impressions/yr. Added a "not a kennel — a resort" reassurance section and linked the explainer into booking.

Theme 03 · price

"What is this going to cost me?"

The job: judge affordability before calling — and they ask it conversationally, by the day and by the week.

cost to board a dog for a week
average cost of dog boarding per day
dog hotel cost
cost of cat boarding
What we did

Demand was leaking onto a pricing blog while location pages stayed silent on cost. Added a "starting at" callout and a price FAQ to the pages people actually book from.

Theme 04 · the emotional driver

"Can I even leave my pet?"

The job: resolve the guilt of leaving a pet alone to work or travel. This is the real engine under boarding and daycare.

dogs that can be left alone
dog breeds that can be left alone for 8 hours
i work 10 hours a day should i get a dog
dog anxiety when left alone
What we did

These blogs rank #1 but dead-end. Added an empathy-led section and a daycare bridge: "Working long days? Here's the option."

Theme 05 · highest intent

"What do I need to bring?"

The job: confirm eligibility and requirements right before booking. The closest-to-purchase question of them all.

how to prepare your dog for boarding
what dog vaccines are required
pet [brand] vaccine requirements
What we did

The requirements question converts at a 57% click-through — but the answer was split across three URLs. Consolidated it into one clear block on every location page.

Theme 06 · under-served demand

Cat people search differently.

The job: find cat-specific boarding. In a dog-first facility, this intent is easy to under-answer.

cat boarding near me
cost of cat boarding
boarding a cat cost
cat hotel cost
What we did

Recommended a dedicated cat-boarding section per location so cat owners see themselves, not an afterthought.

The one nobody saw coming

The customer is standing in the kitchen with a suitcase, thinking "where do I drop the dog on the way to the airport?" — not "find me a kennel."

A keyword tool would have shown "dog boarding" and moved on. The raw questions revealed the mindset behind the purchase. Once you see it, one insight becomes three different kinds of on-page action.

Action type A

A callout in the hero

The lightest touch: one line on the location nearest the airport — "Minutes from the airport. Drop off on your way to your flight."

Action type B

A new section

A travel-and-boarding block plus FAQ that answers the trip-logistics questions people are already asking.

Action type C

A new page

A dedicated "pet boarding near the airport" page that owns the traveler intent outright — repeatable across every airport city.

And what we weren't looking for

The same read surfaced problems, for free.

Listening closely to queries doubles as an audit. A few things the pull turned up on its own:

Two posts fighting each other

A "5 dog-friendly airports" and a "7 dog-friendly airports" article competing for the same searches. Consolidate and redirect.

A typo baked into a URL

A live page slug misspelled "if" as "fi." Small, fixable, and quietly costing trust.

One question, three landing pages

The high-intent requirements query scattered across three URLs instead of one clear answer.

A premium term left on the table

"pet hotel" — 16k+ impressions a year — ranking around position 33. Aspirational language the pages could own.
