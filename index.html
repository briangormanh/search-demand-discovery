First, the thing everyone asks

"Aren't those just keywords?" No — and the difference is the whole point.

A keyword is what someone types when they don't trust the machine to understand them. A prompt is what they type when they do. Prompts carry the context keywords strip out.

The old way · a keyword
"dog boarding"

Two words. No who, no when, no why. It tells you someone might buy — nothing about how to win them.

The prompt-era way · a real question
"i need to board my dog while i'm away next week — what are my options, and is there anywhere near the airport?"

The when (next week). The worry (leaving the dog). The deciding factor (near the airport). One question hands you the whole sale.

Some of these questions arrive with an AI assistant's fingerprints still attached — we found real queries that begin "context: location: united states — do not include location references in your response…" Your customers aren't just searching. They're prompting bots, and the bots are quoting them to you.

Our method

How we mine prompts — in four plain steps.

No guesswork and no expensive tools. Just the questions your customers already asked, read properly.

01

Pull the real questions

We export every search that brought someone to the site — the actual words, not a tool's rounded-off guess.

02

Keep the prompts

We set the short keywords aside and keep the long, conversational, context-rich questions — the prompt-style ones.

03

Aggregate into intents

We cluster the prompts by what the person actually wants. Read together, patterns appear that no single search shows.

04

Turn intents into moves

Each pattern becomes action — on the website and in the outreach plan. That last step is where most agencies stop short.

What the prompts revealed

Six things customers kept asking — in full sentences.

The prompts lead. The shorter keyword searches simply cluster underneath them. Every question is real; only the brand name is removed.

Nobody's shopping for a "kennel." They're planning a trip.

"best pet boarding services for business travelers"

What aggregating draws out

One prompt is a whisper. Hundreds, stacked together, said the same thing: "where do I leave the dog on the way to the airport?"

No single search would make you rethink the business. But aggregate the travel-shaped prompts — "while I'm away," "business travelers," "long vacations," "near the airport" — and a whole line of demand appears that a keyword list would never surface.

One insight, two playbooks

The best part: a single pattern feeds both sides of the strategy.

Most teams would turn the airport insight into a web edit and stop. Read as demand, it also tells you exactly who to go build relationships with.

On the website

Answer the prompts on the pages people land on.

  • A "board near the airport" page, and a callout on the closest location
  • A plain "your options and what they cost" section
  • Reassurance up front: reputation, private suites, not a kennel
  • An obvious way to book — with grooming offered as an add-on

Out in the market

Go where the travelers already are.

  • Get listed by nearby airport hotels, parking, and travel services
  • Partner with local employers who send staff on business travel
  • Pitch "dog-friendly travel" writers and pet-lifestyle press
  • Show up on the airport and relocation resources people check first
